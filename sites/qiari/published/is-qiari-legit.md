# Is Qiari Legit? An Honest Look at the Platform

Qiari is a legitimate subscription app offering personalized remote frequency wellness sessions, built by David Wong (founder of the Qi Coil ecosystem). It's not a medical treatment and doesn't claim to cure or treat disease. Legitimacy here means a transparent business model, a real user base, a functioning platform, and honest positioning as a wellness experience rather than a clinical intervention.

That answer probably raises more questions than it settles. "Legitimate" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and depending on what you mean by it, the answer shifts. If you're asking whether Qiari is a real company with a working product and paying customers, yes. If you're asking whether it's a clinically validated medical treatment, no, and neither Qiari nor any other frequency wellness app on the market is. The honest version of "is Qiari legit" requires separating those two questions and answering each one cleanly.

## What Qiari Actually Is (And What It Isn't)

Qiari is a subscription app that delivers personalized remote frequency sessions through your phone or desktop. You pick an intention (sleep, focus, calm, energy, and so on), and the platform delivers a structured session experience tied to that intention. It's part of a broader ecosystem that includes hardware products like the Qi Coil, but Qiari itself is the device-free, app-based entry point. You can read a fuller breakdown in [what Qiari.ai is and how it fits into the ecosystem](https://www.qiari.ai/blog/what-is-qiari-ai).

### The difference between a wellness app and a medical device

A medical device goes through FDA clearance, makes specific health claims, and gets evaluated by regulators against efficacy and safety standards. A wellness app does not, and Qiari is squarely in the second category. That doesn't make it illegitimate. Meditation apps, breathwork apps, sleep-sound apps, and a huge chunk of the consumer wellness market sit in the same bucket. The honest framing is that Qiari is a structured wellness practice you do through your phone, not a treatment.

### Why Qiari doesn't claim clinical efficacy

This is where a lot of frequency wellness brands get themselves in trouble. The FTC, ASA, and Health Canada have all taken action against companies in the broader quantum and frequency space that made health claims they couldn't back up. Qiari's positioning sidesteps that entirely by not making those claims. Sessions are experiences. Users report what they notice. Nothing is being treated, cured, or diagnosed. That restraint is, paradoxically, one of the strongest signals of legitimacy in this category.

There's no claim that a more expensive tier will produce a stronger biological effect, because the company doesn't claim biological effects in the first place. Higher tiers give you more variety, more advanced session formats, and (at the practitioner level) tools for working with clients. The "AI" part of Qiari is genuine but worth defining. The platform uses pattern recognition to adapt session recommendations to what you've selected, what time of day you tend to use it, and what intentions you've been working with. It's not a chatbot pretending to be a healer. It's a recommendation and scheduling layer on top of a session library, which is a pretty standard piece of modern app infrastructure. ## What Real Qiari Users Report (And How to Evaluate It)

This is the part of any "is X legit" question that gets messy. Anecdotal reports are not proof of anything in a scientific sense, and a smart skeptic will dismiss them. But they're also not nothing, and dismissing them entirely is its own kind of dishonesty.

### Subscriber feedback: the patterns people mention

The recurring themes in subscriber feedback tend to cluster around subjective experience: feeling calmer during sleep sessions, noticing a shift during focus sessions, treating the daily ritual as a structured pause in an otherwise chaotic day. People rarely describe Qiari as a miracle. They describe it as a wellness habit that gives them ten or twenty minutes of intentional state-shifting they wouldn't otherwise carve out. Whether you find that valuable depends on whether you're already someone who finds structured contemplative practices useful.

### Why anecdotal experience isn't the same as clinical proof

A user saying "I sleep better with Qiari" is real data about that user's experience. It's not evidence that Qiari biologically improves sleep across a population. Those are different statements, and conflating them is exactly what gets wellness brands into regulatory trouble. The legitimate version is: people consistently report subjective experiences they find valuable, and Qiari provides a structured framework for those experiences. That's a defensible claim. "Qiari improves sleep" is not.

## The Regulatory Reality of Frequency Wellness Apps

### Why the frequency wellness space gets scrutinized

Frequency wellness sits at a strange intersection. On one side, there's legitimate scientific work on bioelectromagnetic fields, vibrational influence on biological systems, and sound therapy research. On the other side, there's a flood of bad actors making outlandish claims about quantum healing devices that cost thousands of dollars. Regulators paint with a broad brush, and any brand in the space gets some of that paint regardless of how careful their messaging is. This is the context any honest evaluation of Qiari has to sit inside.

### How Qiari stays compliant with FTC and ASA guidelines

The compliant posture is the one Qiari takes: don't make medical claims, don't promise specific health outcomes, describe sessions as experiences, include clear disclaimers, and let users report their own subjective results. If you read Qiari's marketing carefully, you'll notice this language discipline throughout. That's not a workaround. That's the actual line between wellness experience and unapproved medical claim, and staying on the right side of it is part of what makes the platform legit in the regulatory sense.
