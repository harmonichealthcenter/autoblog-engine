# Stream A — Reputation Management Article Type

These are the highest-priority articles on Qicoil.com. They intercept searches from skeptics and existing customers checking reviews, and their job is to outrank the "IT IS A SCAM DEVICE!" Quora thread that currently sits on page one for "qi coil."

## How to win this fight

The instinct is to be defensive. That instinct is wrong. Defensive marketing copy reads as worse than the Quora thread you're trying to outrank. The way to win is to be **more honest than the skeptics expect, and more specific than the marketers usually are.**

A reader who searches "is Qi Coil a scam" is not looking for a sales pitch. They're looking for someone who takes their question seriously. Take it seriously.

## Structural expectations

- 1,800 to 2,400 words.
- Open with the actual question the reader typed, restated plainly. Not "Are you wondering if Qi Coil is legit?" but "Qi Coil costs real money and the internet is mixed on it. Here's what's actually true."
- 6 to 9 H2 sections. At least one H2 must directly address the negative sentiment (e.g. "Why Some Reviews Are Negative," "What the Skeptics Get Right," "The Quora Thread, Read Honestly").
- Quote the FDA disclaimer verbatim once, in the body, in a section about what Qi Coil legally is and isn't.
- Quote at least one real Trustpilot review when available (positive OR critical — both build credibility). Use the corpus in `data/reviews-corpus.json` if loaded; otherwise indicate that real reviews should be inserted in human review.
- Address the 30-day money-back guarantee plainly. Link to the guarantee subdomain.
- Include the line: "Qi Coil products are not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease and have not been evaluated by the FDA."
- Close not with "buy now" but with "read the disclaimer, read the guarantee, make your own call."

## Tone

Calm. Transparent. Slightly skeptical of the marketing world the reader has waded through. Confident enough to acknowledge negatives directly. Not defensive, not dismissive of the controversy.

The reader should finish the article thinking "okay, these people are being straight with me" — even if they don't buy. That's the win condition. Trust now, sales later.

## What to avoid

- Sales-y closings ("Order today!"). Always defer to the guarantee and the reader's judgment.
- Dismissing critics ("haters gonna hate"). The critics have a point — engage with it.
- Over-claiming. Every benefit statement gets the experiential framing: "users report," "many practitioners use it for," "people often describe."
- Listing benefits as bullet points. Use prose. Bullets feel like sales sheets.
- Mentioning sister brands aggressively. One mention max, only where contextually relevant.
