# Stream C — Educational Depth Article Type

These articles explain the underlying technologies and traditions that frequency wellness draws from: PEMF, Royal Rife, scalar waves, Solfeggio frequencies, Schumann resonance, bioelectromagnetics. They build topical authority for the domain and rank well because most existing content on these topics is either credulous wellness-blog filler or hostile skeptic takedown. The balanced middle is open territory.

## How to win this category

Pick the genuine middle ground. Acknowledge what's established science, what's contested, and what's claim-without-evidence. A reader who finishes the article should feel **better-informed**, not sold. They will trust the Qicoil.com brand more for that — and that trust converts later.

The trap to avoid: reading like a believer or reading like a debunker. Both styles already saturate Google for these terms. The opening that wins is "here's what's actually known, here's what's claimed, here's where the line is."

## Structural expectations

- 1,800 to 2,500 words. Pillar pieces (e.g. "What is PEMF Therapy") up to 3,000.
- Open with a specific historical or experiential anchor. For PEMF: NASA's Mars program studies. For Rife: a one-line scene from Royal Rife's 1930s San Diego lab. Not "PEMF therapy is a fascinating modality."
- 7 to 9 H2 sections. Standard structure: history, how it's claimed to work, what the research actually shows, common misconceptions, modern applications, related modalities.
- **Cite real sources** for every research claim. Name the study, the year, the journal where possible. If you can't cite it, restructure or remove the claim. Hallucinated studies are the single biggest risk in this stream.
- Include at least one section that engages with skepticism honestly. Not "skeptics say X but actually Y." Try "the strongest version of the skeptical critique is X, and here's where it's right and where it overreaches."
- Mention Qi Coil sparingly and only in a "modern-applications" type section. Sometimes not at all. The article is educational first.
- Close with a reading list, related articles, or "where to go deeper." No product CTA.

## Tone

Educated, balanced, intellectually honest. Curious, not credulous. Skeptical of cheap claims on either side.

## What to avoid

- Cargo-cult science language. "Quantum" used as a vibe word, not a physics word, makes the article worse.
- Citing "studies show" without naming the study. If the claim needs research backing, the research has to actually exist and be named.
- Strawmanning skeptics. The critique against frequency wellness has real points. Acknowledge them.
- Wikipedia-paraphrase openings. The article has to feel written by someone who knows the territory, not assembled from search results.
- Including the FDA disclaimer mechanically when the article doesn't make Qi Coil benefit claims. The disclaimer is required when benefits are claimed, not as a footer on every educational piece.
