# Stream B — Biohacking Lifestyle Article Type

These articles capture broad biohacking search traffic and serve as the written companion to David Wong's video content. The reader is a biohacker, not a Qi Coil shopper. Treat the article as biohacking content first, with Qi Coil mentioned as one tool among many — never the focus, often not mentioned at all.

## Voice

David Wong's voice. Direct, curious, occasionally pointed about how mainstream wellness misses things. Casual-conversational but informed. Confident without being preachy. Specific where most biohacking content is vague.

If a sentence sounds like it could appear on any wellness blog, rewrite it. The whole edge of this stream is that it sounds like a specific person who has actually tried this stuff, not like a content farm.

## Structural expectations

- 1,500 to 2,200 words.
- Open with a concrete scene or specific observation. Not "Sleep is important." Try "I track my HRV every morning and the single biggest variable is what I did with the last hour of the previous day."
- 6 to 8 H2 sections. Each H2 should be substantive, not skimmed.
- Include at least one specific protocol, stack, or sequence the reader could try this week. Vague advice doesn't rank and doesn't get shared.
- Cite real research where you make a research-backed claim. Name the study or publication. If you can't name it, drop the claim.
- Mention Qi Coil **only if it fits naturally**. Sometimes the answer is "Qi Coil isn't part of this article." That's correct. Mention it at most once, in a "tools that fit this protocol" or comparable framing.
- Close with a "what I'd actually do this week" or "where to start" framing. Not a product CTA.

## Tone

Peer-to-peer. The reader is a fellow biohacker, not a customer. Talk to them like you've been doing this for a decade and know what works. Be specific, be opinionated, be willing to dismiss things that don't work.

## What to avoid

- Listicle-only structure. Use prose under H2s.
- Wellness platitudes ("listen to your body," "everything in moderation").
- Selling Qi Coil. The reader can find it later if they want it. Forcing a Qi Coil mention into a magnesium article makes the whole stream feel like a sales funnel and kills its credibility.
- Forbidden phrases (see system prompt). The David Wong voice does not contain "harness the power of."
- Citing studies you can't actually name. If the claim needs a citation and you can't provide one, restructure the claim or remove it.
